Overview

The UCSC Immunobrowser enables powerful insights into the current state of an individualâ€™s adaptive immune system. Populations of T-cells can be tracked by the unique genetic sequence signature present in the Beta chain of the specialized T-cell Receptor (TCRB). These signatures are extracted from blood samples using state of the art sequencing techniques. Billions of datapoints are transformed into a concise and interactive browser that provides repertoire level snapshots of an individual, while also preserving the power to interrogate individual TCRB sequences. It is capable of identifying immunogenic challenges in an individual, identifying the populations of T-cells actively fighting infection and leverages the entire expanse of scientific literature for clues to its disease specificity.

Immune System

The immune system fights off foreign invaders using two components: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system. The innate immune response is the first line of defense during an infection. Its specialized proteins and cells rapidly target pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) which are shared by common infectious agents. The adaptive immune response generates a response specific to each invading pathogen. Subsequent infections with the same pathogen will illicit a stronger and faster adaptive response.

Adaptive Immunity

Specificity, diversity, memory and self-nonself recognition are the cornerstones of the adaptive immune system. The adaptive immune system is capable of generating a specific response to a diverse array of foreign invaders. A foreign invader that is recognized by the adaptive immune system is known as an antigen and can be bacterial, viral or of other origin. The adaptive immune system also has a memory of past infections. The adaptive immune system generally takes from 5-7 days to begin its first response to a foreign antigen. However the response to a subsequent exposure can occur in as little as 2-3 days and is greater in intensity. Given the ability of the adaptive immune system to mount effective antigen-specific responses, it is amazing that the immune system can recognize and avoid the large number of self-antigens present within the human body.

Although many cell types are involved in the adaptive immune response, B-cells and T-cells play major roles in the adaptive immune system. Each B-cell and T-cell produces a receptor with highly variable structures that have the capacity to bind selectively to an antigen. These receptors are generated by a random process known as VDJ recombination and initial specificty to an antigen is purely due to chance. B-cells are responsible for humoral immunity, i.e. they produce antibodies that can neutralize invaders and mark them for phagocytosis by other leukocytes. T-cells activate B-cells leading to antibody production and are also able to kill self-cells that are infected by pathogens.

In order for a T-cell to carry out these specific functions, it must be activated. Activated T-cells undergo clonal expansion, generating many genetically identical T-cells that can carry out different functions. Clonal expansion of a T-cell can be tracked by its unique receptor sequence and is indicative of an active immune response.

T-cells

Both T-cells and B-cells recognize specific antigens through their receptors which share many similarities. Whereas B-cells can bind directly to a pathogen, T-cells must bind to a complex of digested antigenic peptide bound to an MHC complex. They are divided into two major populations: T-helper cells (TH) and Cytotoxic T-cells (TC). TH cells express a surface protein (CD4) that binds to MHC class II peptides when presented on specialized antigen presenting cells (APC) such as B-cells, dendritic cells and macrophages. TH cells activate B-cells and regulate the immune response by producing cytokines. TC cells express CD8, a receptor that binds to MHC class I peptides when presented on most nucleated cells. TC cells can kill self cells that have been infected by pathogens, such as viruses or bacteria.

T-cell Receptor

All T-cells express a T-cell Receptor (TCR), a membrane bound protein that is composed of a dimer of two protein subunits. The vast majority of T-cells (~95%) express an alpha-beta TCR, whereas the remaining T-cells express a gamma-delta TCR. The role of gamma-delta TCR expressing T-cells have yet to be elucidated. Here we concentrate on T-cells expressing the alpha-beta TCR, whose functions have been well studied and make up the TH and TC populations.

The receptor itself is structurally similar to an antibody and when fully recombined is a member of the antibody-fold superfamily. The portion with antigenic specificity is encoded in three complementarity determining regions (CDR) and is flanked by four framework regions. CDR's 1 and 2 are encoded directly in the human genome. However, CDR3 is generated during development by a process known as VDJ recombination.

VDJ Recombination

Recombination is the molecular process that gives B-cells and T-cells their diversity. In the process of recombination, each B-cell and T-cell precursor undergo genome-level rearrangement of multiple gene segments to create a novel somatic DNA sequence. The rearranged DNA sequence defines the shape of its receptor. Recombination contains multiple steps including combinatorial junction joining, junctional flexibility, P-addition, and N-addition. When combined with the joining of heavy and light chains, the resulting possible number of sequences in the repertoire is estimated to be between 10^15-10^20 for T-cells and is similarly large for B-cells.

Combinatorial junction joining is the process in which V, D and J segments are joined together randomly. Each V and J segment contain conserved sequences for the formation of the immunoglobulin fold, but differ significantly at the sequence level. Combinatorial joining of individual V, D and J segments account for 2.25 x 10^6 and 5.29 x 10^6 possible combinations in BCRs and TCRs. TCR-Î² and IgH chains recombine V, D and J units while TCR-Î±, IgÎ» and IgÎº recombine only the V and J segments.

During the joining of V, D, or J segments, genetic sequence may be altered by deletion or by addition of palindromic sequence at the junctions of a segment (P-addition). These additions are a result of the joing process that creates a hairpin at the end of each segment and creates a nick randomly. Following the creation of a nick, random nucleotides are added by Terminal deoxyribonucleic acid transferase. These deletions and additions account for much of the diversity found in recombined TCRs.
