Overview

The UCSC Immunobrowser enables powerful insights into the current state of an individualâ€™s adaptive immune system. Populations of T-cells can be tracked by the unique genetic sequence signature present in the Beta chain of the specialized T-cell Receptor (TCRB). These signatures are extracted from blood samples using state of the art sequencing techniques. Billions of datapoints are transformed into a concise and interactive browser that provides repertoire level snapshots of an individual, while also preserving the power to interrogate individual TCRB sequences. It is capable of identifying immunogenic challenges in an individual, identifying the populations of T-cells actively fighting infection and leverages the entire expanse of scientific literature for clues to its function.

Immune System

The immune system fights off foreign invaders using two components: the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system. The innate immune response is the first line of defense during an infection. Its specialized proteins and cells target pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) which are shared by common infectious agents. The innate immune response is rapid and targets the same PAMPs during each challenge. The adaptive immune response generates a response specific to each invading pathogen. Subsequent infections with the same pathogen will illicit a stronger and faster adaptive response.

Adaptive Immunity

Specificity, diversity, memory and self-nonself recognition are the cornerstones of the adaptive immune system. The adaptive immune system is capable of generating a specific response to a diverse array of foreign invaders. A foreign invader that is recognized by the adaptive immune system is known as an antigen and can be bacterial, viral or of other origin. In addition to the ability to specifically recognize antigens rather than common molecular patterns, the adaptive immune system has a memory of past antigens. The adaptive immune system generally takes from 5-7 days to begin its response to a foreign antigen. However, upon second exposure of an antigen, the reaction can occur in as little as 2-3 days and the response is greater in intensity. Given the ability of the adaptive immune system to mount effective antigen-specific responses, it is amazing that the immune system can recognize and avoid the large number of self-antigens present within the human body.

Although many cell types are involved in the adaptive immune response, B-cells and T-cells play major roles in the adaptive immune system. Each B-cell and T-cell produces a receptor with highly variable structures that have the capacity to bind selectively to an antigen. B-cells are responsible for humoral immunity, i.e. they produce antibodies that can neutralize invaders and mark them for phagocytosis by other leukocytes. T-cells are a major player in cell-mediated immunity.

T-cells

T-cells are restricted to binding to peptide-MHC complex and are divided into two major populations: T-helper cells (TH) and Cytotoxic T-cells (TC). TH cells express CD4, a receptor that binds to MHC class II peptides when presented on specialized antigen presenting cells (APC) such as B-cells, dendritic cells and macrophages. TH cells activate B-cells and regulate the immune response by producing cytokines. TC cells express CD8, a receptor that binds to MHC class I peptides when presented on most nucleated cells. TC cells kill self cells that have been infected by pathogens, such as viruses or bacteria. Both T cells and B cells recognize specific antigens through their receptors which share many similarities.
